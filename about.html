Neuroscience, Theoretically
I am currently a research associate at the Allen Institute for Brain Science and also a masters student in applied mathematics at the University of Washington. My research interests center on the intersection between in vivo and in silico computation through the lens of mathematics especially regarding non-parametric approaches in conjunction with high-throughput modalities like calcium imaging and high-density probes; after all, the brain - like the layers in an artificial neural network - is just factorizing and projecting information into subsequently lower-dimensional and salient subspaces (I think?).

Experience

Research Associate II - Neural Coding Department

The Allen Institute for Brain Science

Conducted the in vivo imaging of the mouse visual cortex (V1 and higher visual areas) during a passive viewing experiment with intrinsic signal imaging and 2-photon calcium imaging of Cre-positive, GCaMP6f mice. These experiments — a part of the Brain Observatory — served to investigate the layer-dependent and areal retinotopic organization of the mouse visual cortex from a single-cell, functional perspective. Different layers of excitatory neurons were explored along with sub-populations of inhibitory neurons. The results of this work revealed that many neurons in purportedly "simple" visual areas encoded for higher-order features and that relatively few neurons encoded gabor-like receptive fields. The bioRxiv preprint can be found here. I also helped to characterize the inter-subject versus the inter-experiment differences in intrinsic signal imaging experiments; the bioRxiv preprint can be found here.

Continuing on the previous successes of the Brain Observatory, our experiments transitioned to a behavioral go/no go visual discrimination task in which the same neurons were imaged over a dozen times as the mouse was presented with trained and novel natural images. I also helped to collect data for the Allen Institute's OpenScope project which is an initiative to allow external collaborators to propose projects to be conducted using Allen Institute experimental infrastructures.

Beyond my role as an experimentalist, I also helped deliver meta-analyses of our data collection and developed automated QC systems using a combination of Python and SQL. This helped increase the throughput of our data collection and reduce the inter-operator subjectivity with regards to quality control. I also helped drive the development of a murine eye tracking system from both a hardware and algorithmic perspective which led the completion of a key SMART goal. As a result, I was asked to present my work and data product at a Society for Neuroscience poster session. I am currently involved in the next iteration of the eye tracking system which leverages machine learning techniques in scene segmentation.
